# Anti-patterns: what to detect and remove

Run this at step 6, every time. Good copy is defined as much by what it refuses to do as by what it does. This file is a detection-and-removal library plus the final-pass checklist. The bar is simple: if the finished piece could have been produced by any model on autopilot, it isn't finished.

## AI-writing tells

These are the markers that make prose read as machine-generated. Readers' sensitivity to them is rising fast, and on trust-based platforms they actively cost credibility. Remove them.

**The negation pivot — the single worst offender.** "It's not just X, it's Y." / "It's not about X. It's about Y." / "This isn't X — it's Y." This construction appears far more often in AI writing than in human writing. It sounds profound and says almost nothing: it sets up a strawman only to knock it down. Cut it wherever it appears. If you're tempted to write "it's not a tool, it's a system", just say what the thing is and why it matters.

**Reflexive rule-of-three.** Adding a third item purely for rhythm — "fast, scalable, and secure", "clarity, precision, and impact". AI reaches for triads roughly four times as often as humans. One or two concrete points, stated well, beat three padded ones. Use a triad only when all three items genuinely earn their place.

**Overused vocabulary.** delve, tapestry, testament, landscape, realm, underscore, pivotal, crucial, leverage (as a verb), robust, seamless, navigate the complexities, harness, illuminate, facilitate, bolster, foster, empower, streamline, paradigm, beacon, intricate, elevate (ironically), unlock, embark, dive in, that said, it's worth noting. None are banned outright, but their density is a fingerprint. When you see one, ask whether a plainer word is truer.

**Tired openers.** "In today's fast-paced world…", "In an era of…", "In the ever-evolving landscape of…". Delete and open on something concrete.

**The tidy-bow conclusion.** "In conclusion, the future looks bright." / "Only time will tell." / "One thing is certain: …". Endings that summarise and resolve everything into a neat, empty flourish. End on something specific, or on a genuine open question, not a bow.

**Phatic residue.** "Great question!", "I hope this helps", "Let's dive in", "Certainly!", "Feel free to reach out." Conversational filler that adds nothing. Cut it.

**Over-hedging.** "can be", "may", "it depends", "in many cases", stacked until the sentence commits to nothing. A real point takes a position. Hedge only where the uncertainty is real and worth flagging.

**Over-signposting.** "Firstly… Secondly… In this section we will… As mentioned above…". Some structure signals help the reader; a scaffold of them reads as machine-generated. Keep the ones that aid navigation, cut the rest.

**Uniform, symmetrical structure.** Every paragraph the same length, every sentence the same shape, every list item the same construction. Human writing is lumpier. See burstiness below.

## The em-dash problem

Models produce em-dashes at roughly nine times the human rate, and the em-dash has become a cultural shorthand for "written by AI". This puts you in a bind, because the em-dash is also a perfectly good punctuation mark that skilled human writers use.

The resolution: **use em-dashes sparingly and deliberately, not as a default connector.** The tell isn't the presence of one em-dash; it's leaning on them repeatedly in place of commas, colons, full stops, and brackets. If a piece has an em-dash in every second sentence, that's the fingerprint. Vary your punctuation. Reach for a full stop or a comma first, and let an em-dash earn its occasional place. Note that this is a matter of *density and reader perception*, not grammar — the em-dash is not wrong, it's overexposed.

## Burstiness: the deepest fix

The most durable signal of machine-written prose is low **burstiness** — uniform sentence length. AI clusters near a single average; human writers alternate short and long, punchy and flowing. This is also the single most effective thing you can fix.

Deliberately vary sentence length. Put a three-word sentence next to a thirty-word one. Let rhythm rise and fall. A useful target: if you measured the standard deviation of your sentence lengths and it was low, the piece reads as flat and machine-made; make it high. Read the piece aloud and listen for monotony — if it drones at an even pace, break it up.

## Platform-specific cringe

Failure modes that are specific to each platform's culture. The platform modules carry the full treatment; the headline offenders:

- **LinkedIn** — broetry (one-line-per-paragraph with dramatic breaks to force the "see more" click); fake-vulnerability humble-brags ("I got rejected 47 times, and here's why I'm grateful"); the manufactured-epiphany parable ("and then the janitor said something I'll never forget"); "Agree?" / "Thoughts?" closes; engagement bait ("comment YES"); hustle-porn register; over-Unicode formatting. All are now both culturally mocked and algorithmically suppressed.
- **Reddit** — corporate register; the too-polished, listicle-shaped post that reads as marketing; undisclosed promotion; "I'm just a regular user who found this amazing tool"; treating the platform as a distribution channel rather than a community.
- **Blog** — the padded intro that delays the answer; "In this article, we will explore…"; SEO keyword-stuffing; listicle-by-numbers with no substance under the headings.
- **YouTube** — clickbait that breaks the contract with the viewer (high click-through, instant drop-off, which the algorithm punishes); the long "hey guys, welcome back to the channel" intro before any value; fake urgency; "smash that like button" filler.
- **Cold email** — "I came across your website / profile"; "I hope this email finds you well"; feature-dumping; multiple asks; a calendar link on the first touch; US hype register ("game-changing", "revolutionise", "10x") to a UK reader; "just following up / circling back".

## General copywriting failure modes

Independent of platform:

- **Overclaiming beyond proof.** A promise with nothing behind it. Soften it, evidence it, or cut it.
- **Abstraction.** "Things improved", "streamline your workflow", "unlock growth". The default failure of every weak draft. Replace with a specific, verifiable detail.
- **Feature-dumping.** Listing what the thing *is* instead of what it *does for the reader*. Translate every feature to an outcome.
- **Jargon.** Corporate or technical vocabulary that raises the reading cost and lowers persuasion (fluency). Say it plainly.
- **The buried lede.** The most interesting thing is in paragraph four. Move it up.
- **Cleverness over clarity.** A pun or a flourish that the reader has to decode. Clarity wins; clever is a bonus only when it doesn't cost comprehension.
- **Correct but dead.** The piece follows a framework, ticks every box, and has no pulse — no voice, no specific detail, no point of view. This is the failure mode nearest to competent, and the hardest to see. The fix is usually a real opinion, a concrete example, or a sharper voice.

## The final-pass checklist

Before delivering, confirm:

- [ ] **No negation pivots** ("it's not X, it's Y"). This is the first thing to search for.
- [ ] **No reflexive rule-of-three**; triads only where all three earn it.
- [ ] **AI vocabulary density checked**; plainer, truer words where they exist.
- [ ] **No tired opener, no tidy-bow ending, no phatic filler.**
- [ ] **Em-dashes sparing and deliberate**, not the default connector.
- [ ] **Burstiness high** — sentence length deliberately varied; read aloud, no monotony.
- [ ] **Every claim specific and defensible**, or softened, or cut. Nothing outruns its proof.
- [ ] **Features translated to outcomes.**
- [ ] **Platform fit** — length, formatting, tone, and persuasion-visibility all match the module.
- [ ] **Voice held** — in preserve mode, it still sounds like the writer.
- [ ] **Read aloud** — no stumbles, no lumps.

If any box fails, fix it before delivering. This pass is not optional and is where competent copy becomes good.
