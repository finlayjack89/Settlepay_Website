# Cold email to UK SMEs

**Essence:** a cold email is unambiguously a pitch, so the whole game is respecting the reader's time and earning a reply. Short, plain, genuinely relevant, understated. It also carries a real legal and deliverability layer that the copy must not break. Read this whole module before drafting any outreach.

The worked context is SettlePay — a UK software vendor (not a regulated financial firm; it doesn't handle money) selling branded payment pages and invoicing/reconciliation automation to UK SMEs — but the technique and compliance apply to any UK B2B cold outreach.

## The compliance layer (get this right first)

Two UK regimes apply at once. **PECR** governs whether you may *send* the message; **UK GDPR** governs whether you may *use the person's data* to send it. This is guidance to build into the copy and targeting, not legal advice; for a specific list or campaign the user should take advice.

**The critical distinction — who you're emailing:**
- **Corporate subscribers** (limited companies, LLPs, PLCs, public bodies): under PECR you may send B2B marketing email *without prior consent*. This is what makes UK cold B2B email lawful.
- **Individual subscribers** (sole traders, ordinary/unincorporated partnerships, anyone at a personal address): treated like consumers — they generally require consent. Cold-emailing them without it is where senders get caught.
- **The practical problem:** you often can't tell from an email address whether the recipient is a limited company or a sole trader. The safe rule, and the ICO's own guidance: **if you're unsure, treat them as an individual subscriber** — verify status at list-build where it matters (a Companies House check), and segment sole traders and partnerships out of no-consent sends.

**UK GDPR still applies even to corporate subscribers**, because a named work address (jane.smith@company.co.uk) is personal data. So the sender needs:
- **A lawful basis — legitimate interests** — with a documented Legitimate Interests Assessment (the balancing test: a genuine commercial interest, cold email as a proportionate means, not overriding the recipient's reasonable expectations — which for relevant, easy-to-opt-out B2B email it usually doesn't).
- **Transparency** — a privacy notice, linked in the email; if the data came from public sources, the person must be informed within about a month.
- Note: a true role address (info@, sales@) with no identifiable individual is not personal data, so UK GDPR doesn't bite there — but PECR still classifies the subscriber.

**Every cold email must:**
- **Identify the sender clearly** — real name, company, and a valid contact/postal address in the footer.
- **Give a simple opt-out**, and honour it immediately. For a named individual the right to object is absolute. Keep a suppression list and screen every send against it.
- **Use an honest subject line** — no fake "Re:" or "Fwd:", no deception.

The penalty for getting the individual-subscriber or GDPR layer wrong rose sharply under the Data (Use and Access) Act 2025 (PECR fines aligned with UK GDPR levels), so this is not a corner to cut. Enforcement is complaint-driven — annoyed recipients are the trigger — so relevance and easy opt-out are protective as well as decent.

**The copy's compliance furniture, every time:** clear sender identity, a plain-text opt-out line (e.g. "Not the right person? Just reply and I'll stop"), an honest subject, and a privacy notice available. Bake these in rather than bolting them on.

## Deliverability: what the copy must not sabotage
Infrastructure (authentication, domain warm-up, volume caps, list quality) decides most of whether email lands, and that's set up separately. But the copy can wreck it. Respect these:
- **Default to plain text.** Image-heavy HTML signals a marketing blast and bounces far more. Minimal formatting.
- **At most one link, ideally zero on the first touch.** Introduce links only in later, engaged touches. No link shorteners.
- **No attachments on the first touch.**
- **No ALL CAPS, no "!!!", no spam-trigger phrasing, no fake "Re:".**
- **Turn off open/click tracking for cold first touches** — tracking pixels and wrapped links hurt deliverability, and post-Apple-Mail-Privacy the open data is unreliable anyway.
- **Keep it short** — long walls read as bulk.
- **Vary the copy** so no two sends are identical; identical templates create a bulk fingerprint. Genuine personalisation is a deliverability asset, not just a persuasion one.

## Substantiating claims defensibly
SettlePay is a software vendor, so financial-promotion rules don't apply — but the advertising rules (ASA/CAP) do. Any objective, provable-sounding claim ("saves you X hours", "get paid X days faster") needs evidence tied to the exact wording. So:
- Prefer **scoped, qualified claims** over broad numeric promises: "in a pilot, teams cut manual reconciliation steps by up to half" beats "cut your admin in half."
- **Attribute** any hard figure to a named case or source ("per FSB…", "in our pilot with…").
- Use outcome framing the reader can evaluate ("designed to cut the time you spend chasing invoices") rather than an unqualified guarantee.
- Puffery and obvious opinion are fine ("invoicing should be painless"); provable-sounding statistics without evidence are not.

## The anatomy of the email
A flexible model, not a rigid template. Total around 50–100 plain-text words:
1. **Subject** — 3–7 words, lowercase, honest, tied to the signal or pain.
2. **Opener (one line)** — about *them*, led by a real observation or trigger. Never "I came across" or "I'm X from Y".
3. **Relevance / problem (one to two lines)** — the felt SME pain in their terms, matched to the reader's role.
4. **Value / proof (one to two lines)** — one outcome plus one specific, substantiated proof point. Features translated to outcomes.
5. **The ask (one line)** — single, low-friction, interest-based on a first touch.
6. **Sign-off + compliance furniture** — real name, company, valid contact address; plain-text opt-out line.

## Subject line and preview
- **Short**: three to seven words, under ~40–50 characters so it doesn't truncate on mobile.
- **Lowercase / sentence case** tends to beat title case — it reads like a person, not a campaign.
- Tie it to the observed signal or the felt pain ("chasing overdue invoices?", "getting paid at [Company]").
- Avoid hype, "free", ALL CAPS, and emojis for finance and senior audiences.
- **Optimise for replies, not opens** — Apple Mail Privacy Protection auto-loads pixels, so open rate is meaningless now.
- In a plain-text email the **first line is the preview**, so the opener does double duty — make it earn the open.

## The opener
The biggest lever after targeting. Why the standard openers fail: "Hi, I'm X from Y" leads with the sender, whom the reader doesn't care about yet; "I came across your website/profile" is pattern-matched as a cold template within seconds. Both signal mass-send.

The pattern that works: **observation, then implication.** "Saw [specific fact about their business] and figured [specific implication for them]." Reference a real trigger or operational reality, then connect it to the reason for the email. Specificity that proves genuine research beats generic flattery. Avoid "hope this finds you well", "congrats on the new role", and every other line the reader has read a hundred times.

## Body frameworks
Lead with the reader's problem or outcome, never features. Pick by context:
- **PAS (compressed)** — name a felt pain, sharpen it, resolve it. Best when the pain is acute and known (chasing overdue invoices).
- **BAB** — current painful state, better state, your product as the bridge. Best for outcome-led selling (cash flow, admin time).
- **"Why you / why now"** — tie a specific trigger to timeliness. Best when there's a real signal (a new finance hire, expansion, a new tool).
One idea per email. Save detail for after the reply.

## Length
50–125 words is the repeatedly validated sweet spot; 50–75 for senior or finance readers scanning on a phone. The test: *would I reply to this from a stranger, on my phone?* If it needs scrolling, it's too long.

## The ask
One ask. On a first touch, a **soft, interest-based ask beats a hard meeting request** — asking for interest books more meetings than asking for time, because a calendar request cold triggers loss aversion. Examples that work cold: "Worth a quick look?", "Open to me sharing how?", "Is [problem] on the radar this quarter?", "Want me to send the one-pager?" Hold specific meeting asks, calendar links, and the website link for once the prospect has engaged. Escalate the ask across a sequence rather than repeating it. For SettlePay's varied goals (book a call / open a conversation / visit the site), default the first touch to the softest version.

## Personalisation
The distinction that matters is superficial mail-merge ({FirstName}, {Company}) versus genuine relevance that proves research. Three tiers, ascending in power:
- **Firmographic** (sector, size, location, tech stack) — scalable, moderate relevance.
- **Observed pain** (a felt operational problem for their role and sector) — higher.
- **Trigger events** (funding, hiring, expansion, a new tool, a new finance hire) — highest, because they signal timing and receptivity.
Resolve the "personalisation at scale" tension by researching one genuine relevance hook per prospect (or tight segment) while keeping the value proposition templated. The frame to encode: **observed signal → implication for them → how the product maps to that implication → soft ask.** Broken merge tags do the opposite of personalisation — check them.

## Positioning for a payments / invoicing product
Translate features into felt SME pains for a finance or operations reader:
- Branded/hosted payment pages → get paid faster, fewer excuses not to pay, look professional at the point of payment.
- Invoicing automation → less time on admin, fewer errors.
- Reconciliation automation → stop manually matching payments to invoices, close the books days sooner.
Anchor on getting paid, cash flow, admin time, and error reduction — what the buyer actually feels. Late payment is a real, evidenced UK SME pain (roughly half of UK SME invoices are paid late), which gives outreach a legitimate hook — but attribute the figures ("per Sage/FSB…") rather than implying they're SettlePay's own measured results. Differentiate on branding/ownership and reconciliation without asking the prospect to switch their bank or payment provider, and don't disparage competitors.

Match the message to the reader's role: **owner-manager** feels cash flow and time personally (lead with getting paid, less admin); **finance manager** feels reconciliation load and month-end close (accuracy, control, time); **bookkeeper** feels manual matching and chasing daily (fewer manual steps); **operations** feels process friction (efficiency, fewer errors).

## UK register
UK B2B email is more understated than US. Over-the-top hype ("game-changing", "revolutionise", "10x your revenue!!!") reads as untrustworthy to UK readers, who are attuned to lazy outreach and respond to relevance, evidence, and a little personality over hard selling. Be confident and conversational, not hyped and not stiff. Avoid both "Dear Sir/Madam" formality and false US familiarity. British spelling throughout ("optimise", "organisation", "£"). Understatement over superlatives.

## Sequences and follow-ups
Most replies come from follow-ups, not the first email. Practical cadence:
- **Three to four follow-ups** over about two to three weeks (a 3-7-7 rhythm — day 0, +3, +10, +17 — captures most replies). More than about five risks complaints and diminishing returns.
- **Each follow-up adds a new angle or value** — a different proof point, a different pain, a genuinely useful resource. Never "just bumping this up" or "circling back".
- **Vary the structure** across the sequence: touch one is trigger/relevance plus a soft ask; touch two is a specific value or proof point; touch three is a different angle or a resource; the final is a **break-up email** ("I'll close the loop — if getting paid faster becomes a priority, I'm here"), which reliably earns a disproportionate share of replies through loss aversion.
- Escalate the ask across the sequence (soft → specific → calendar) as engagement appears. Stop at an explicit no or after the break-up; re-approach in a few months.

## Realistic expectations
So the copy isn't blamed for structural limits: average B2B cold reply rates run about 3–5%, and B2B SaaS sits at the low end (roughly 2–4%); meetings booked run about 0.5–2.5% of sends. Small, tightly-targeted lists beat large blasts. If a segment falls well below this, the problem is almost always targeting or deliverability, not the words — fix those first.

## The tells to avoid
"I came across…"; "hope this finds you well"; feature-dumping; obvious or broken merge tags; generic flattery; multiple asks; a calendar link or website link on the first touch; US hype register for a UK reader; unsubstantiated specific savings claims; spam-trigger structure (images, multiple links, attachments, ALL CAPS, "!!!", fake "Re:"); and "just following up".
